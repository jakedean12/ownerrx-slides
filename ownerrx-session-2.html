Part 1 · ~20 min

From Request to Problem

What someone tells you is never the whole story. This is how you find the real thing underneath.

The first thing anyone tells you about a problem is a request, not a problem. A request tells you what they want built. A problem tells you why it matters, who it hurts, and what "fixed" actually looks like.

Request
"We need an agent that summarizes our client calls."

That's what someone says. The problem underneath it might be:

"Partners spend 4 hours a week writing recap emails after calls. 30% of those emails go out late or get skipped — which makes clients feel dropped. That's a retention problem, not a writing problem."

Now you know what to build, who feels the pain, and what "fixed" looks like. The request was "build a summarizer." The problem was "partners are losing the thread on follow-ups."

Three questions that get you from request to problem

1
Who feels the pain, specifically? A name and a role, not "the team." If you can't name one person who goes home on Friday frustrated because of this problem, the problem isn't real yet.
2
What does the current bad version look like? What is actually happening today that shouldn't be? Be concrete — hours per week, errors per month, things that get missed, decisions that take too long.
3
What does "fixed" look like? One measurable thing. Not "more efficient." Not "better." Something you could count in 60 days and know whether you moved it.

Exercise — Apply the three questions now

Think about what you heard from the guest speaker yesterday. Pick one problem they described. Apply the three questions out loud — your facilitator will write answers on the board.

If you can't answer all three: you don't have a problem yet. You have a starting point for better questions. Write down what's missing — those gaps are what you'd ask the business owner next time.
The output of this exercise: one sentence per question. Three sentences total. That's the seed of your problem statement — you'll develop it fully in homework.
Part 2 · ~30 min

Design an Approach You Can Defend

The path you chose, the paths you rejected, and the first thing that could go wrong.

Once you have a problem, there are usually three to five ways to solve it. Your job is to pick one — and be able to explain why you picked it over the others. That's a defensible approach.

A defensible approach has exactly three parts:

1
The path you chose — what you're going to build and how it solves the problem you defined
2
The paths you rejected — what you considered and why they lose. This is the part most people skip. Don't skip it.
3
The first risk — the single thing most likely to go wrong, and what you'd do about it
Why the rejected paths matter: They show you thought about it. They prevent the meeting where someone says "but did you consider X?" and you have to admit you didn't. And they leave a record for whoever picks up the work after you.

See it in practice

Using the call-summary problem from Part 1:

Chosen path
A Claude Code agent that drafts recap emails from call transcripts

Partners get a draft in their inbox within 5 minutes of a call ending. They review, edit in their voice, send. Total time: under 5 minutes per call instead of 20–30.

Rejected paths
Two alternatives considered

A Zapier automation: Loses. Partners want to edit before sending — Zapier would send without review. One bad auto-sent email to a client kills the whole experiment.

A part-time VA: Loses on cost and speed. A VA adds a person to the loop when the constraint is the loop itself.

First risk
Transcript quality varies — bad input produces unusable output

Mitigation: start with partners who use a transcription tool with reliable output (Fireflies, Otter). Don't try to fix transcript quality in v1 — validate the workflow first.

Exercise — Draft your approach

Using the problem you defined in Part 1, draft your three-part approach on paper or in a notes app. You have 10 minutes. It doesn't need to be polished — it needs to be real.

  • One sentence on the path you're choosing
  • Two approaches you're rejecting and why each loses
  • One sentence on the biggest risk

When time is up, one volunteer reads theirs out loud. The room asks: "Does this defend itself?"

Part 3 · ~25 min

Claude as Your Thought Partner

Three prompts that stress-test any approach before you commit to building it.

You have a draft approach. Now use Claude to pressure-test it. Claude is not going to build anything in this part — it's going to think alongside you.

Open Claude Code in your kp-academy folder:

cd ~/kp-academy
claude

Prompt 1 — Generate alternatives you might have missed

Paste your problem statement, then ask:

Here's a business problem: [paste your problem statement in one paragraph].

What are three different ways an AI agent could solve this? For each approach, describe what it does, what it would cost in time to build, and one situation where it would fail.

Read what Claude says. Did it surface an approach you hadn't considered? If yes — is it better than yours, or does yours still win? Add the best new option to your rejected-paths list.

Prompt 2 — Find the first failure

Describe your chosen approach, then ask:

Here's the approach I'm planning: [describe your chosen path in 2-3 sentences].

What is the single most likely reason this fails in the first 30 days? Be specific — not "technical issues" but the exact thing that breaks.

If Claude names a risk you hadn't thought of, update your first-risk section. If it names the same one you had, your instinct was right.

Prompt 3 — Write the one-paragraph memo

Now ask Claude to draft the deliverable:

Based on everything above, write a one-paragraph approach memo I could put on a business owner's desk. Include: what I'm building and why, what I considered and rejected, and the first risk I'm watching. Write it in plain English, no jargon, one paragraph.
This paragraph is the artifact. It's what you'd send a sponsor before starting a build. Save it — you'll refine it in homework and it becomes part of your operator portfolio.

Share back

Pick two people to read their approach memo paragraph out loud. The room listens for: Is the problem clear? Does the chosen path defend itself? Is the risk honest?

When listening to another pair's memo: Ask "does this defend itself?" Good feedback sounds like: "I don't understand who feels this pain" or "why does option B actually lose?"
Part 4 · ~15 min

The Three Modes — Choosing How Claude Thinks

Default, Plan Mode, and Thinking Mode. One question tells you which to use.

Claude Code has three ways of operating. The question that picks the right one: "Am I about to change something, or am I asking Claude to think?"

ModeWhen to use itHow to activate
Default Research, questions, explanations — anything that doesn't write or change files Just type your prompt
Plan Mode Any time Claude is about to write, edit, or delete something. It shows you the plan first — you approve before it acts. Press Shift+Tab before your prompt — or type /plan
Thinking Mode Hard decisions, ambiguous problems, architecture choices — situations where reasoning out loud leads to a better answer Start your prompt with think hard —
The most common mistake: using Default mode when Claude is about to write files. Default mode acts immediately — if Claude misunderstood your prompt, the file is already changed. Use Plan Mode any time files are involved.

Exercise — Run your approach memo through all three

Use the business problem you've been working with today. Run the same prompt in three modes and notice what changes.

Run 1 — Default: Just paste your problem and ask Claude what agent would solve it.

Based on this problem: [your problem statement]. What kind of AI agent would solve this?

Run 2 — Plan Mode: Press Shift+Tab first, then ask Claude to save the approach memo to a file.

Save my approach memo to a file called approach-notes.md in the current folder. Here's the content: [paste your memo paragraph].

Plan Mode shows you what it's about to write before writing it. Review the plan — if it looks right, approve it.

Run 3 — Thinking Mode: Add "think hard" and ask Claude to evaluate your chosen approach vs the alternatives.

think hard — Here are three approaches to solving [your problem]: [list them]. Which approach is most defensible given that the business owner wants [what they care about]? Show your reasoning.

After all three runs: which mode felt most useful for consulting work vs building work? That's the intuition you're building.
